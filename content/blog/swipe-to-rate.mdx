The final score is a liar. Not about who won, it gets that right. It lies about whether the game was any good. A 31-28 thriller and a 31-28 snoozefest read identically on the box score. One of them had four lead changes and a goal-line stand. The other was 28-3 with garbage time inflating the loser. The scoreboard cannot tell you which was which. Nothing in the entire sports-media stack can tell you which was which, not the spread, not the highlight package, not the recap headline.

That gap is the whole reason swipe-to-rate exists. Buzzr asks a question the scoreboard refuses to: **was this worth your two hours?** And the answer comes out as a single number between 1 and 10, the Buzzr Score, built on entertainment, not outcome.

The interesting part is not the number. It's how you give it. You swipe.

## The friction problem nobody solved

Here's the thing about asking fans to rate games. They will do it exactly once, in a burst of enthusiasm, and then never again, if you make it cost anything. A star rating widget with a "submit" button and a confirmation modal is three taps and a decision. Multiply that by the eleven games on a Saturday slate and you have built a chore. Chores don't get done.

Every rating product that came before fell into the same trap. They treated the rating like a form submission, a discrete transaction with a beginning, a middle, and a "thanks for your feedback." Forms are where intent goes to die. The gap between "I have an opinion about this game" and "I have logged an opinion about this game in your database" is where you lose 90% of people.

The fix is not a better form. The fix is no form. A gesture you already know, that your thumb already does a thousand times a day, that carries the verdict and commits it in one motion. That is what the swipe is for. It collapses opinion and action into the same physical event.

## What the swipe actually does

Open the deck and you get one card at a time, full screen. A live game. A take from someone you follow. A breaking-news card. A highlight pulled from a game you already cared about. The Buzz Score sits right on the game card so you see the verdict before you decide what to do with it.

Then your thumb does the talking. Internally the whole system runs on a tiny type, `SwipeDirection = 'right' | 'left'`, and that binary is the entire vocabulary. **Right is a commit.** Swipe a game right and it lands on your wishlist, the app calls `setMyInterest` with intent `wishlist` and a confirmation banner slides up with an Undo button already wired. **Left is a pass.** Scroll past and the card is gone, logged as a skip, feeding the signal quietly. No modal. No submit. No "are you sure."

The right-swipe is deliberately heavier than the left. When you commit, the phone answers back with a medium haptic, a firmer thump than the light tick a skip gives you. That is not decoration. Commit-style actions earn the stronger tactile weight on purpose, so your hand learns the difference between "yes, this one" and "next" without your eyes leaving the card. The gesture has texture. It feels like a decision because it is one.

And because a single wrong flick should never cost you anything, every commit is reversible. The confirmation banner holds exactly one action at a time, so a fast-swiping fan on a twelve-game night never stacks a tower of toasts, and the Undo button can always reverse the last thing your thumb did. Low stakes is the point. Low stakes is what keeps you swiping.

## Why entertainment is the right axis

You could rate a game on a lot of things. Quality of play. Star power. Defensive discipline. We rate it on one: did it entertain you. That choice is the soul of the product, and the swipe is built to serve it.

Entertainment is a gut call. You don't need a spreadsheet to know a triple-overtime Game 7 was a 10 and a 40-point blowout with the starters resting was a 3. You knew it while it was happening. The swipe meets that instinct where it lives, in the gut, in the thumb, in the half-second after the buzzer when you turn to whoever is next to you and say "that was unreal" or "that was unwatchable." It does not ask you to justify the score. It asks you to register the feeling before it fades.

That is why the Buzzr Score lands in bands, not just digits. **Peak. Great. Good. Mid. Bad. Garbage.** A band is a feeling with a name. When you swipe, you are not solving for a decimal, you are pointing at a band you already feel, and the system handles the math underneath. The number is exact. The band is what you remember and what you text a friend.

## The swipe is a vote, and votes compound

One person swiping is an opinion. Ten thousand people swiping is a signal nobody else has. Every commit and every skip is a `card_interaction`, a vote, and those votes pile up into something the scoreboard can never produce: a live, crowd-sourced read on which games are actually worth your night.

This is the quiet payoff. The same gesture that costs you nothing builds the discovery layer for everyone. You swipe right because you want the game on your wishlist. As a side effect, you just told the room it was good. Multiply that across a Saturday and the Buzz Score stops being one algorithm's guess and becomes a verdict the crowd helped reach. Skip Game 5, rave about Game 6, and three months later when someone asks "was that series any good," there is an answer to point at instead of a shrug.

The swipe also teaches the feed. Swipe a highlight right and similar content gets boosted in the Swarm. Swipe it left and it gets demoted. The gesture is doing double duty, registering your verdict on this game and tuning what the next card will be. You are training your own feed with the same flick that logs your opinion, and you never had to open a settings menu to do it.

## One gesture, the whole thesis

Strip Buzzr down to a single sentence and it is this: the scoreboard rates outcomes, we rate experiences, and the swipe is how a feeling becomes a number without ever feeling like work.

That is not a small claim. The reason fans never had a real entertainment rating for games is not that nobody thought of the idea. It is that nobody made it cheap enough to do every night, for every game, on instinct. Forms were too expensive. Stars-and-submit was too expensive. The swipe is free, in the only currency that matters, your attention and your thumb.

We did not invent the verdict. You have been delivering it for years, out loud, to whoever was on the couch next to you. We just gave your thumb a way to log it, and gave everyone else a way to read it back.
